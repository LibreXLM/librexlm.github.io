To understand why this matters, we need to look at the history of money. For most of human civilization, money was inseparable from the communities that used it. It took the form of shells, metal, paper â€“ each tied to culture, ritual, or state power. With the rise of capitalism and the expansion of financial systems, money became increasingly abstract, reduced to numbers in ledgers and later to digits on screens. It grew detached from daily life, governed by central banks and global markets.

This detachment had consequences. What once served as a tool of trust and cooperation gradually became a cold instrument of accumulation and control. As the money supply expanded and financial systems grew ever more complex, the individual relationship to money weakened. It turned into something faceless, where value is dictated by invisible forces and impersonal institutions. Money was, in a sense, dehumanized.

Personal tokens offer a possible reversal of this trend. They allow money to once again carry meaning, personality, and identity.
